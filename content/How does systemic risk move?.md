
**1. Systemic risk propagates along the reflexivity gap** Risk doesn't just move through balance sheet contagion (bank A fails, bank B loses collateral). It moves through _model failure_ — the moment when the gap between what the system does and what it understands about itself doing it becomes too large to bridge. 2008 wasn't primarily a liquidity crisis, it was a reflexivity crisis. The system's self-model catastrophically failed to capture correlated behaviour across institutions.

**2. Risk is topological, not just volumetric** Following Veloz's COT framework — systemic risk isn't just about how much exposure exists, but about which _organisations_ (stable self-maintaining sub-networks) are viable. When a shock hits, the system doesn't smoothly degrade — it jumps discontinuously between organisational states. Some configurations are genuinely stable, others are transient and collapse rapidly. The risk isn't in the size of positions but in whether the current configuration is a real organisation or a phantom one.

**3. Contagion follows the coarse-graining failure** Jessica Flack's work suggests stable complex systems build representations of themselves at multiple scales simultaneously. A healthy financial system would have adequate self-models at the level of individual institutions, sectors, and the whole system. Systemic risk propagates fastest where coarse-graining fails — where there's no adequate representation at a particular scale. In 2008 that was the sector level — individual banks had models, regulators had aggregate models, but nobody had an adequate model of correlated behaviour within the mortgage-backed securities ecosystem specifically.

**4. Risk has a spanda signature** Following the emergence-precedes-spanda framework — systemic risk accumulates silently during periods of high functional diversity growth without corresponding reflexivity growth. The system is generating new functions (instruments, strategies, counterparty relationships) faster than it can integrate them into its self-model. The spanda of the system — its felt aliveness, its internal coherence — is actually _decreasing_ during apparent boom periods, because the ratio of integrated to unintegrated complexity is falling. The crisis is when that incoherence becomes visible.

**5. Propagation is non-Euclidean** This connects directly to the garden vision. Risk doesn't travel through the financial system in straight lines or even network-shortest-paths. It travels through the topology of _beliefs about beliefs_ — what institution A thinks institution B thinks about institution C's solvency. That's a hyperbolic space, not a flat one. Which means standard network contagion models systematically underestimate propagation speed and reach, because they model Euclidean distances in a non-Euclidean topology.

**6. The scaling coefficient as early warning signal** Here's a postulate worth developing: if reflexivity scales sublinearly relative to functional diversity, the _derivative_ of that gap — how fast it's widening — might be a leading indicator of systemic risk accumulation. During stable periods the gap widens slowly. During boom periods it widens fast. A regulatory framework that monitors this gap in real time would be more informative than VaR or leverage ratios, which are essentially static snapshots of a dynamic topology.
